## Wagoner County Courthouse Information

**Wagoner County Courthouse**  
307 E. Cherokee Street  
Wagoner, Oklahoma 74467  
**Phone:** (918) 485-4508  
**Fax:** (918) 485-5025

The Wagoner County Courthouse serves as the central hub for state court proceedings in the county. Located in downtown Wagoner, this historic facility houses the District Court Clerk's office, where civil cases are filed and court records are maintained. The courthouse is situated at the intersection of Cherokee Street and Main Street, making it easily accessible for attorneys, litigants, and process servers.

**Office Hours:** Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 4:30 PM (closed weekends and federal holidays)

**Parking:** Free public parking is available around the courthouse square, with additional street parking throughout downtown Wagoner.

### Court Jurisdiction

The Wagoner County District Court handles all state-level civil and criminal matters, including:
- Family law cases (divorce, custody, child support)
- Civil disputes and small claims
- Protective orders and restraining orders
- Probate and estate matters
- Eviction proceedings
- Felony and misdemeanor criminal cases

## Communities We Serve in Wagoner County

### Wagoner (County Seat)

With a population of approximately 8,400 residents, Wagoner serves as the county seat and maintains its historic designation as the "Queen City of the Prairie." Founded in 1887 and named after Henry "Bigfoot" Wagoner, a dispatcher for the Missouri-Kansas-Texas (Katy) Railroad, the city preserves its rich railroad heritage through its historic downtown district.

**Notable Locations for Service:**
- Historic Downtown Wagoner – centered around the courthouse square
- Wagoner City Historical Museum
- Wagoner Public Schools district office
- Wagoner Medical Center
- Residential neighborhoods extending along Highway 51

The city hosts annual events including the Chili Bluegrass Festival and Summerfest, which can affect traffic patterns during peak times.

### Coweta

Coweta is one of the fastest-growing communities in Oklahoma, with a population exceeding 10,000 residents. Located in northwestern Wagoner County, approximately 30 minutes from both Tulsa and Muskogee, Coweta has transformed from a quiet agricultural town to a thriving suburban community.

The city's name derives from a Creek Indian war town on the Chattahoochee River in Georgia, with the area first settled by Native Americans around 1840. Today, Coweta maintains its agricultural roots while embracing suburban growth.

**Service Considerations:**
- Coweta Public Schools campuses
- Mission Bell Museum (historic downtown)
- Residential developments along Highway 51
- Commercial corridors near the Muskogee Turnpike interchange

### Broken Arrow

As Oklahoma's fourth-largest city, Broken Arrow extends into the western portion of Wagoner County. With a population exceeding 116,000, this rapidly growing community offers diverse service locations ranging from established neighborhoods to new residential developments.

**Service Areas:**
- Rose District (historic downtown)
- Indian Springs Sports Complex area
- Broken Arrow Public School facilities
- Numerous residential subdivisions and apartment complexes

### Bixby

Known as "The Garden Spot of Oklahoma," Bixby straddles Tulsa and Wagoner counties. The portion within Wagoner County has experienced significant growth, with the city overall reaching approximately 28,600 residents by the 2020 Census. Bixby's agricultural heritage remains strong, with the area serving as a major sod-growing center.

### Porter

Located between Tulsa and Muskogee on State Highway 51B, Porter is a small town with significant historical importance. Named after Pleasant Porter, who later became chief of the Creek Nation, the town was established in 1903 when the Missouri, Kansas and Oklahoma Railroad built through the area.

**Service Considerations:**
- Compact downtown area
- Porter Consolidated Schools
- Residential areas extending from the main corridor
- Agricultural properties surrounding the town

### Okay

Situated on the east bank of the Verdigris River, Okay serves as a gateway community for Fort Gibson Lake recreation. This small town provides access to lake communities and seasonal residences that require specialized service approaches.

### Fair Oaks and Tullahassee

These historic communities represent important parts of Wagoner County's diverse heritage. Fair Oaks provides a rural service environment, while Tullahassee holds significance as one of Oklahoma's oldest historically Black towns.

## Geographic and Demographic Overview

### Population and Growth

According to the 2020 U.S. Census, Wagoner County's population stands at 80,981 residents, making it Oklahoma's seventh-largest county by population. The county has experienced significant growth, expanding from 73,085 in 2010 to nearly 81,000—a growth rate of approximately 10.8%.

As of July 2021 estimates, the population had climbed to approximately 84,050, establishing Wagoner County as one of Oklahoma's fastest-growing counties. Most growth has concentrated on the western side of the county, particularly in the Broken Arrow and Coweta areas.

### Demographics (2020 Census)

- **White:** 66.8%
- **American Indian/Alaska Native:** 10.3%
- **Two or more races:** 10.3%
- **Hispanic/Latino:** 8.6%
- **Black/African American:** 3.3%
- **Asian:** 1.4%

The significant Native American population reflects the county's location within historically significant Creek and Cherokee territory.

### Economic Profile

Wagoner County's median household income is approximately $72,000, with median home values around $200,000.

## Fort Gibson Lake: A Defining Geographic Feature

### Lake Overview

Fort Gibson Lake represents one of Wagoner County's most significant geographic features. Created in 1942 by impounding the Grand (Neosho) River, the lake spans approximately 19,990 surface acres with 225 miles of shoreline. The lake extends into Wagoner, Cherokee, and Mayes counties, with the majority of its recreational facilities accessible from Wagoner County.

### Recreation Areas

**Taylor Ferry** – Located just east of Wagoner on Highway 51, Taylor Ferry offers year-round camping and lake access with numerous private residences and vacation homes.

**Rocky Point** – Situated on the upper end of the lake where Flat Rock Creek meets the main channel, providing camping, boat ramps, and a swim beach.

**Flat Rock Creek** – This campground on the east shore offers views and attracts visitors seeking a more remote lake experience.

**Sequoyah State Park** – Located on a forested point with outdoor recreation including horseback riding and hiking trails.

### Service Challenges

Process serving around Fort Gibson Lake presents unique challenges:

- **Seasonal Residences:** Many lake properties serve as vacation homes
- **Remote Locations:** Lake communities often involve long driveways and limited cellular service
- **Gated Communities:** Several lake-area subdivisions maintain controlled access
- **Weekend vs. Weekday Patterns:** Service success rates vary based on resident schedules

**Arkansas River** – Forms part of the western and southern boundaries.

**Grand River (Neosho River)** – Impounded to create Fort Gibson Lake, flowing through the heart of the county.

## Historical Context

### County Formation

Wagoner County was organized on July 16, 1907, as part of Oklahoma's admission to statehood. The county was named after the town of Wagoner, which won a contested election against Porter and Coweta for the county seat.

### Civil War Significance

During the Civil War, Wagoner County was the site of the Battle of Flat Rock in 1865, where Confederate troops led by Brigadier General Stand Watie captured 85 Union troops.

### Early Settlement

Archaeological evidence indicates Caddoan Mound Builders inhabited the area between 300 and 1200 AD. The western portion was later settled by Creek peoples following their removal from Alabama in the 1820s, while the eastern portion attracted Cherokee settlers.
