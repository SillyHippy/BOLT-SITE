## Overview of Washington County

**Washington County**, located in northeastern Oklahoma along the Kansas border, stands as a testament to American industrial heritage. Named in honor of **President George Washington**, this compact county covers just **424 square miles**—the **second-smallest county in Oklahoma** by both land area and total area. Despite its modest geography, Washington County played an outsized role in American industrial history as the birthplace of Oklahoma's petroleum industry.

As of the **2020 census**, Washington County is home to **52,455 residents**, with **Bartlesville** serving as both the county seat and economic hub. The county is part of the **Tulsa-Muskogee-Bartlesville Combined Statistical Area** and maintains its own micropolitan statistical area. Situated within the **Eastern Lowlands physiographic region**, Washington County is drained by the **Caney River**, with **Copan Lake** and other reservoirs providing recreational opportunities.

The county's geography features rolling hills and tallgrass prairie. The **Oklahoma-Kansas state line** forms the northern boundary, while **Osage County** lies to the west, **Nowata County** to the east, **Rogers County** to the southeast, and **Tulsa County** to the south.

## Historical Significance: The Birthplace of Oklahoma Oil

Washington County's history is inextricably linked to the petroleum industry that transformed Oklahoma from Indian Territory into an industrial powerhouse. The story begins in **1897**, when the **Nellie Johnstone No. 1** well erupted with oil on April 15—the **first commercial oil well in what would become Oklahoma**.

The well was named for **Nellie Johnstone**, the six-year-old daughter of **William Johnstone**, a Delaware Nation businessman who, with his partner **George B. Keeler** (an Osage Nation member), secured mineral leases on over **200,000 acres** of Cherokee Nation land. When the well was "shot" with nitroglycerin, it produced up to **75 barrels of oil per day**. Within a decade, Bartlesville's population exploded from **200 residents to over 4,000**, while Oklahoma's oil production grew from 1,000 barrels to more than **43 million barrels annually**.

The oil boom attracted ambitious entrepreneurs including a young **J. Paul Getty**, who sold the *Saturday Evening Post* in Bartlesville as a boy. But no figure became more synonymous with Bartlesville than **Frank Phillips**, who founded **Phillips Petroleum Company** in 1917. The company grew into an international energy giant, remaining headquartered in Bartlesville until its merger with Conoco in 2002.

Phillips Petroleum's legacy extends beyond business. Frank Phillips built **Woolaroc**—a sprawling ranch retreat that now serves as a world-class museum and wildlife preserve. The **Frank and Jane Phillips House** is listed on the National Register of Historic Places, as is the **Price Tower**—the only skyscraper designed by **Frank Lloyd Wright** ever constructed.

The **Old Washington County Courthouse** at 400 Frank Phillips Boulevard served as the center of county government for decades before the current courthouse was established.

## Communities Within Washington County

Washington County encompasses **two cities** and **four towns**:

### Cities

**Bartlesville** (County Seat) – Population: ~37,000  
The economic, cultural, and governmental center of Washington County. Home to the county courthouse, major healthcare facilities, **Oklahoma Wesleyan University**, and corporate offices. Downtown features the iconic **Price Tower** and a thriving commercial district.

**Dewey** – Population: ~3,500  
Located just north of Bartlesville, Dewey maintains its own municipal court. The **Dewey Hotel** (1899) is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

### Towns

**Copan** – Population: ~700  
An oil town that emerged during the early 20th century boom. The Prairie Oil and Gas Company built Oklahoma's first trunk pipeline through the area in 1904. The **Copan oil field** had nearly 2,200 producing wells by 1915. Today, **Copan Lake** is a popular recreational destination.

**Ochelata** – Population: ~400  
Northwest of Bartlesville, Ochelata developed as a railroad and oil town served by the Santa Fe Railroad.

**Ramona** – Population: ~500  
Founded as an oil town and Santa Fe Railroad stop. Recent community leadership has worked to revitalize the town with infrastructure improvements.

**Vera** – Population: ~240  
The smallest incorporated community in Washington County, representing the rural agricultural heritage.

Additional unincorporated areas include **Cotton Valley**, **Hogshooter**, **Matoaka**, **Oglesby**, and **Owen**.

## Process Serving in Washington County

### Understanding Service of Process

**Service of process** is the formal procedure by which individuals and entities are notified of legal actions against them. This fundamental component of due process ensures all parties have adequate notice and opportunity to respond. In Washington County, proper service of process is essential for courts to establish jurisdiction.

### Types of Documents Served

Process servers in Washington County regularly handle:

- **Summons and Complaints** initiating civil lawsuits
- **Subpoenas** requiring witness testimony or document production
- **Writs of Execution** and **Garnishments** for judgment enforcement
- **Temporary Restraining Orders** and **Protection Orders**
- **Divorce Petitions** and **Family Law Motions**
- **Eviction Notices** and **Forcible Entry and Detainer Actions**
- **Small Claims Court Documents**
- **Probate and Estate Notices**
- **Corporate Service of Process**

### Geographic Considerations

Washington County's compact geography presents both advantages and challenges. The concentration of population in **Bartlesville** and **Dewey** facilitates efficient urban service, while rural communities require travel on **U.S. Highway 60**, **U.S. Highway 75**, **State Highway 10**, **State Highway 123**, and county roads.

Process servers navigate:

- **Urban residential neighborhoods** in Bartlesville and Dewey
- **Ranch and farm properties** throughout the county
- **Commercial and industrial facilities** in the petroleum sector
- **Multi-story office buildings** in downtown Bartlesville
- **Gated communities** and private developments
- **Apartment complexes** and multi-family housing

The Kansas border location occasionally requires coordination with process servers in **Montgomery County** and **Chautauqua County, Kansas**.

### Service Methods and Requirements

**Personal Service:** Direct delivery to the named party is the preferred and most reliable method.

**Substituted Service:** When personal service isn't immediately achievable, Oklahoma law permits service on a person of suitable age and discretion at the defendant's dwelling.

**Service on Corporations:** Corporations may be served through their registered agent with the Oklahoma Secretary of State or through authorized officers.

**Service by Publication:** When traditional methods fail after diligent effort, courts may authorize publication in a newspaper of general circulation.

### Filing and Documentation

After completing service, process servers must file a **Return of Service** with the Washington County Court Clerk, detailing:

- Date, time, and location of service
- Identity of the person served
- Manner of service
- Relevant observations
