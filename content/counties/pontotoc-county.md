## Ada, Oklahoma: The "City of Champions"

### About Ada

Ada serves as the county seat and largest city in Pontotoc County, with a population of approximately 16,500 residents. Located almost exactly in the center of Pontotoc County from east to west, Ada is eighteen miles from the southern county boundary and six miles north of the Canadian River.

Known as the **"City of Champions,"** Ada has a rich history dating back to its establishment in 1893 by Jeff Reed, a Texas cowboy who named the town after his daughter. The community grew rapidly with the arrival of the railroad in 1900-1901, and by 1904, the population exceeded 3,000.

### East Central University (ECU)

**East Central University** is a cornerstone institution located on a 100-acre campus in Ada, serving approximately 3,500-4,000 students each semester.

ECU offers over 80 undergraduate majors and several master's degree programs. The university is particularly noted for:

- **Legal Studies Degree** – One of the few programs of its kind in Oklahoma

Notable ECU alumni include two Oklahoma governors and Ernest McFarland, who became Governor of Arizona and a U.S. Senator.

### Chickasaw Nation Headquarters

**The Chickasaw Nation is headquartered in Ada**, making Pontotoc County a central hub of tribal governance and commerce. The Chickasaw Nation's jurisdiction spans **7,648 square miles across 13 counties in southern Oklahoma**, including all of Pontotoc County.

**Key Facts About the Chickasaw Nation:**
- One of the largest federally recognized tribes in the United States
- Governed by a constitution adopted in 1856 at Good Springs
- Tribal capital historically located in Tishomingo (named after Chief Tishomingo)
- The reservation was functionally affirmed by the U.S. Supreme Court in the *McGirt v. Oklahoma* decision (July 2020)
- Current leadership: Governor Bill Anoatubby and Lt. Governor Chris Anoatubby

The presence of the Chickasaw Nation headquarters creates a unique legal landscape in Pontotoc County, as certain criminal and civil matters may fall under tribal, federal, or concurrent jurisdiction depending on the parties involved and the nature of the case.
