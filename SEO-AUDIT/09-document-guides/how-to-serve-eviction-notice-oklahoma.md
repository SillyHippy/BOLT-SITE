# How to Serve Eviction Notices in Oklahoma: Landlord Guide

Serving eviction notices in Oklahoma requires strict adherence to state statutes and proper procedures. Whether you're a landlord in Tulsa County, Creek County, or anywhere else in Oklahoma, understanding how to properly serve eviction notices is essential for protecting your property rights and ensuring your case proceeds through the legal system without unnecessary delays.

This comprehensive guide covers everything you need to know about serving eviction notices under **41 O.S. § 131** (Forcible Entry and Detainer), including notice types, service methods, and post-service procedures.

## Understanding Oklahoma Eviction Law: 41 O.S. § 131

### The Forcible Entry and Detainer Statute

Oklahoma evictions are governed by the **Forcible Entry and Detainer Act**, codified in **Title 41, Section 131** of the Oklahoma Statutes. This law establishes the procedures landlords must follow to legally remove tenants from rental properties.

Key points about **41 O.S. § 131**:
- Evictions must be handled through the court system
- Self-help evictions (changing locks, shutting off utilities, removing tenant property) are illegal
- Proper notice is required before filing an eviction lawsuit
- Service must follow specific statutory requirements

## 5-Day Notice vs. 10-Day Notice in Oklahoma

### 5-Day Notice to Quit (Non-Payment of Rent)

Under **41 O.S. § 131(B)**, when a tenant fails to pay rent, the landlord must serve a **5-day notice to quit** (also called a 5-day notice to pay or vacate). Key requirements:

- Must be in writing
- Must specify the amount of rent owed
- Must give the tenant **5 days** to pay the rent or vacate the property
- If the tenant pays within 5 days, the eviction cannot proceed
- If the tenant does not pay or vacate, the landlord may file for eviction on day 6

**Important:** The 5-day period excludes weekends and holidays in some jurisdictions. Check with your local court clerk for specific requirements in Tulsa County and Creek County.

### 10-Day Notice to Cure or Quit (Lease Violations)

For lease violations other than non-payment of rent, Oklahoma law generally requires a **10-day notice to cure or quit**:

- The tenant has **10 days** to correct (cure) the violation
- If the violation is cured within 10 days, the lease continues
- If not cured, the tenant must vacate
- Landlord may file for eviction after the 10-day period expires

Common lease violations triggering the 10-day notice include:
- Unauthorized pets
- Unauthorized occupants
- Property damage
- Noise violations
- Other lease term breaches

### 30-Day Notice to Terminate (Month-to-Month Tenancies)

For month-to-month tenancies where no specific violation has occurred, landlords must provide a **30-day written notice** to terminate the tenancy under **41 O.S. § 111**.

## Proper Notice Requirements for Oklahoma Evictions

### What Must Be Included in an Eviction Notice:

1. **Property address** - The complete rental property address
2. **Tenant name(s)** - All adult tenants named on the lease
3. **Landlord/Agent name and contact information**
4. **Reason for eviction** - Non-payment, lease violation, or termination
5. **Amount owed** (if applicable) - Including late fees (if allowed by lease)
6. **Deadline** - 5 days, 10 days, or 30 days depending on notice type
7. **Statement of tenant's rights** - Reference to cure or quit options
8. **Date of notice**

### Tulsa County and Creek County Specifics

Both Tulsa County and Creek County follow Oklahoma state law for eviction notice content. However, local court clerks may have specific formatting preferences:

**Tulsa County District Court:**
- Eviction cases are typically filed in the Special Judge docket (under $10,000)
- Notice should be clear and unambiguous
- Keep copies of all notices served

**Creek County:**
- Evictions filed at the Creek County Courthouse in Sapulpa
- Follow state notice requirements
- Sheriff's Office or licensed process server can serve notices

## Service Methods for Eviction Notices

### Who Can Serve Eviction Notices in Oklahoma?

Unlike formal court process, **eviction notices** (the pre-filing notices) can be served by:
- The landlord
- The landlord's agent or property manager
- A professional process server
- The sheriff's department

However, once the eviction lawsuit is filed, **service of court papers** must follow **12 O.S. § 2004** and be performed by:
- Sheriff or deputy sheriff
- Licensed private process server (under 12 O.S. § 158.1)
- Court-appointed individual

### Acceptable Service Methods for Pre-Filing Notices:

#### 1. Personal Delivery

Handing the notice directly to the tenant is the most effective service method:
- Provide to any adult occupant of the rental unit
- The tenant cannot refuse service by not accepting the document
- If tenant refuses to take it, it can be left at their feet
- Document the date, time, and manner of service

#### 2. Substituted Service at the Property

If the tenant is not home, the notice may be:
- Left with a person **15 years or older** residing at the property
- Posted on the door of the rental unit (in some jurisdictions)
- Mailed to the tenant's last known address

**Note:** Posting requirements vary. Some jurisdictions require both posting AND mailing.

#### 3. Certified Mail

Sending the notice by certified mail with return receipt requested:
- Provides proof of delivery
- Tenant's signature on return receipt confirms receipt
- If tenant refuses certified mail, it may be considered valid service in some cases
- Keep the return receipt and mailing receipt

### Best Practices for Service Documentation:

Regardless of the method used, document everything:
- **Date and time** of service attempt
- **Method** of service used
- **Person served** (name and description)
- **Witness information** (if anyone witnessed the service)
- **Photographs** (if posting on door is permitted)
- **Return receipts** (if mailed)

## Commercial vs. Residential Eviction Notices

### Residential Evictions

Residential evictions in Oklahoma are governed by **41 O.S. § 131** and include:
- Single-family homes
- Apartments
- Duplexes
- Mobile homes (in some circumstances)

**Key Considerations:**
- Residential tenants have stronger legal protections
- Habitability requirements must be met
- Retaliatory eviction protections apply
- Proper notice is strictly enforced

### Commercial Evictions

Commercial property evictions generally follow the same **41 O.S. § 131** procedures but with some differences:

**Key Differences:**
- Commercial leases may specify different notice periods
- Self-help eviction may be permitted in some commercial contexts (check lease terms)
- Tenant's personal property may be handled differently
- Courts may move faster on commercial evictions

**Important:** Even with commercial tenants, landlords should follow statutory procedures to avoid liability.

## After Service: Next Steps in the Oklahoma Eviction Process

### Step 1: Wait for the Notice Period to Expire

- **5-Day Notice:** Wait 5 days (excluding weekends/holidays if applicable)
- **10-Day Notice:** Wait 10 days for tenant to cure or vacate
- **30-Day Notice:** Wait 30 days for month-to-month terminations

### Step 2: File the Eviction Lawsuit (Forcible Entry and Detainer)

If the tenant has not cured the violation or vacated after the notice period:

1. **File with the Court Clerk** in the county where the property is located
   - Tulsa County: 500 S. Denver Ave., Tulsa, OK
   - Creek County: 222 E. Dewey Ave., Sapulpa, OK

2. **Required documents typically include:**
   - Petition for Forcible Entry and Detainer
   - Copy of the lease agreement
   - Copy of the eviction notice served
   - Proof of service of the notice
   - Filing fee (varies by county)

3. **Filing fees:**
   - Tulsa County: Contact Court Clerk for current fees
   - Creek County: Contact Court Clerk for current fees
   - Generally ranges from $50-$150 depending on the claim amount

### Step 3: Service of Court Documents

After filing, the tenant must be served with the court summons and petition under **12 O.S. § 2004**:

- Service by sheriff or licensed private process server
- Personal service preferred
- Substituted service permitted (with resident 15+ years old)
- Court hearing is typically scheduled 5-10 days after filing

### Step 4: Court Hearing

**At the hearing:**
- Both landlord and tenant present their cases
- Bring all documentation (lease, notices, proof of service, payment records)
- The judge will issue a ruling

**Possible outcomes:**
- Judgment for landlord (eviction granted)
- Judgment for tenant (eviction denied)
- Dismissal (procedural errors)
- Continuance (rescheduled for more evidence)

### Step 5: Writ of Execution (If Eviction Granted)

If the court grants eviction:
- Landlord receives a **Writ of Execution**
- The sheriff will schedule the physical removal of the tenant
- Tenant typically has **48 hours** to vacate after the writ is posted
- Sheriff oversees the physical eviction

## Frequently Asked Questions About Eviction Notices in Oklahoma

### Can I hand the eviction notice to the tenant myself?

**Yes.** Unlike court process, pre-filing eviction notices can be served by the landlord. However, many landlords prefer to use a professional process server for documentation purposes.

### What if the tenant refuses to accept the eviction notice?

If the tenant refuses to physically accept the notice, you can:
- Leave it at their feet (personal service still accomplished)
- Post it on the door (if local rules permit)
- Mail it certified with return receipt
- Use a process server who can document the refusal

### Do weekends and holidays count in the 5-day notice period?

In many Oklahoma jurisdictions, weekends and holidays are excluded from the 5-day notice period for non-payment evictions. However, practices vary by county. Contact your local court clerk for specific guidance in Tulsa County and Creek County.

### Can I include late fees in the amount due on a 5-day notice?

Late fees can be included **only if** they are specified in the written lease agreement. Arbitrary late fees not agreed to in the lease may not be enforceable.

### What happens if the tenant pays partial rent during the 5-day notice period?

Accepting partial rent can complicate the eviction. Generally:
- If you accept partial payment, you may need to restart the process
- Some judges view partial payment as a waiver of the eviction
- Consult with an attorney before accepting partial payments during eviction

### Can I evict a tenant without a written lease?

Yes. Oral (verbal) leases are valid in Oklahoma, though harder to prove. For month-to-month tenancies without a written lease, a 30-day notice to terminate is typically required.

### Is it legal to shut off utilities or change locks to force a tenant out?

**No.** Self-help evictions are illegal in Oklahoma. Landlords who:
- Shut off utilities
- Change locks
- Remove tenant belongings
- Harass tenants

May face civil liability and criminal penalties under **41 O.S. § 121**.

### What if the tenant claims the property is uninhabitable?

Tenants may raise a "constructive eviction" defense if the property has serious habitability issues. Landlords should:
- Maintain properties in habitable condition
- Make necessary repairs promptly
- Document all maintenance efforts

### Can I evict a tenant for having an emotional support animal?

Federal fair housing laws protect tenants with disabilities who have legitimate emotional support animals. However, tenants must:
- Provide proper documentation from a healthcare provider
- Follow reasonable pet rules (cleanup, noise, etc.)
- Obtain the animal through proper channels

### How long does the entire eviction process take in Oklahoma?

From notice to physical eviction typically takes:
- **Fastest:** 2-3 weeks (uncontested, no delays)
- **Average:** 4-6 weeks
- **Contested:** 2-3 months or longer if appeals are filed
