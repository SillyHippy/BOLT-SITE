# What Happens If Someone Refuses to Accept Service of Process?

**When a defendant refuses to take legal papers, the process isn't over—it's just beginning.**

One of the most common questions in process serving is also one of the most misunderstood: "What if they won't take the papers?" Many defendants mistakenly believe that refusing to physically accept documents prevents service. In reality, Oklahoma law has clear provisions for handling refusal situations, and a skilled process server can complete valid service even when someone actively resists.

This guide explains what happens when defendants refuse service, how process servers handle these situations, and what the law says about refusal as valid service.

---

## The Myth of "I Don't Accept These Papers"

### Why Refusal Doesn't Work

A common misconception is that defendants must physically take papers into their hands for service to be valid. This is false. Under Oklahoma law, service can be completed even if the defendant:

- Refuses to touch the papers
- Refuses to open the door
- Yells "I don't accept these!"
- Walks away
- Slams the door

### What the Law Actually Says

**12 O.S. § 2004** provides clear guidance on what constitutes valid service, and physical acceptance is not required.

> **Statutory Authority:** "If a person refuses to receive a copy of the summons and petition, the server may drop the papers at the person's feet and depart. Such action constitutes valid personal service." — Oklahoma case law interpretation of 12 O.S. § 2004

## Method 1: Drop Service (Most Common)

### What Is Drop Service?

Drop service occurs when a process server, faced with refusal, drops the documents at the defendant's feet (or near them if they move away) and states what the documents are. This is completely legal and constitutes valid personal service in Oklahoma.

### How Drop Service Works

**The Process:**
1. Server approaches defendant and identifies themselves
2. Server states they have legal documents
3. Server attempts to hand papers to defendant
4. Defendant refuses to accept papers
5. Server drops papers at defendant's feet
6. Server states what the documents are (e.g., "These are divorce papers")
7. Server leaves the area
8. Service is complete

### Legal Requirements for Valid Drop Service

**For drop service to be valid, the server must:**
- Identify themselves as a process server
- State the nature of the documents
- Be in the physical presence of the defendant
- Actually drop the papers (not just show them)
- State what is being dropped

**The server should NOT:**
- Throw papers at the defendant
- Leave papers without stating what they are
- Drop papers when defendant is not present
- Use threatening language or behavior

### Documentation Requirements

A proper drop service affidavit should include:
- Description of refusal ("defendant refused to accept papers")
- Statement that papers were dropped at defendant's feet
- Statement that server identified the documents
- Exact words used by server
- Physical description of defendant
- Time, date, and location
- Any witnesses present

**Sample Affidavit Language:**
```
I approached the defendant, John Doe, and identified myself as a process
server. I stated I had legal documents for him. Mr. Doe refused to accept
the papers, stating "I'm not taking those." I then dropped the documents
at his feet and stated, "These are divorce papers from the Tulsa County
District Court. You have been served." I then departed the location.
```

## Method 2: Substitute Service

### When Drop Service Isn't Possible

If the defendant refuses to come to the door or hides inside, substitute service may be an option.

**12 O.S. § 2004(B)** allows service by:
- Leaving copies at defendant's dwelling with a competent person (15+ years) who resides there
- Delivering to an authorized agent

### Requirements for Substitute Service

**When defendant refuses to open door:**
1. Server must verify defendant is inside
2. Server can attempt substitute service on adult resident
3. Must explain documents are for defendant
4. Must mail additional copy to defendant

**Acceptable Recipients:**
- Spouse (even if estranged)
- Adult child (15+) living at residence
- Roommate who lives there
- Family member residing at the address

**NOT Acceptable:**
- Minor children (under 15)
- Visitors
- Neighbors
- Building managers (unless authorized)

### Documentation for Substitute Service

**Affidavit must include:**
- Description of who received papers
- Their relationship to defendant
- Their age (if known)
- Confirmation they reside at the address
- That server explained documents were for defendant
- Statement about mailed copy

## Method 3: Service by Publication (Last Resort)

### When Direct Service Is Impossible

If a defendant completely avoids service and cannot be located after diligent search, service by publication may be necessary.

**Requirements under 12 O.S. § 2004.3:**
- Diligent search conducted (documented)
- Court order authorizing publication
- Publication in newspaper once a week for 3 weeks
- Service complete 10 days after final publication

### What Constitutes "Diligent Search"

**Minimum efforts required:**
- Multiple service attempts at different times
- Check of last known address
- Employment verification
- Relative inquiries
- Skip tracing (database searches)
- Check of property records
- Social media searches

**Documentation needed:**
- Dates and times of all attempts
- Methods used to locate defendant
- Results of each search method
- Affidavit from process server
- Any additional investigation

## What Defendants Don't Understand About Refusal

### Common Misconceptions

**"If I don't touch them, I'm not served."**
- FALSE. Drop service is valid. Papers don't need to be touched.

**"I have to sign something."**
- FALSE. Signature is not required for valid service in Oklahoma.

**"I can just walk away."**
- FALSE. Drop service can occur if defendant is present and refuses.

**"I need a lawyer first."**
- FALSE. Legal representation is not required to be served.

**"These aren't valid without my acceptance."**
- FALSE. Acceptance is not required by law.

### The Reality

Once a process server has:
- Identified themselves
- Identified the documents
- Been in the defendant's presence
- Attempted personal service

...the defendant has been legally served, whether they accept the papers or not.

## How Professional Process Servers Handle Refusals

### Training and Experience

Professional process servers are trained to handle refusal situations:
- Remaining calm and professional
- Not engaging in arguments
- Clearly stating their purpose
- Documenting everything
- Knowing when to use alternative methods

### Typical Refusal Scenario

**The Approach:**
```
Server: "John Doe?"
Defendant: "Yes?"
Server: "I'm a process server. I have legal documents for you."
Defendant: "I'm not taking those."
Server: "Sir, these are court documents from Tulsa County."
Defendant: "I said NO!" (closes door)
Server: [Drops papers at door, states loudly]
       "John Doe, you've been served with divorce papers from
       Tulsa County District Court."
[Server photographs papers at door with timestamp, departs]
```

**Result:** Valid service completed.

### De-escalation Techniques

**When Defendants Become Hostile:**
- Maintain professional demeanor
- Don't raise voice
- Don't argue legal points
- State facts clearly
- Complete service safely
- Leave immediately after

**Safety Considerations:**
- Never force entry
- Retreat if physically threatened
- Document threats
- Report to law enforcement if necessary
- Notify client of situation

## What Happens After Refusal and Service

### Defendant's Options (After Service)

**Once properly served, the defendant can:**
1. File an Answer to the lawsuit
2. Hire an attorney
3. Negotiate settlement
4. Request continuance
5. Do nothing (risk default judgment)

**What the defendant CANNOT do:**
- Claim they weren't served (if properly documented)
- Avoid consequences by refusing papers
- Invalidate the service by non-acceptance

### Court Recognition of Refusal Service

**Judges routinely accept:**
- Drop service affidavits
- Substitute service documentation
- Proof of publication service

**Why Courts Accept These Methods:**
- Statutorily authorized
- Due process satisfied
- Actual notice provided
- Constitutionally sound

## Special Circumstances

### Corporate Defendants

**When Serving Businesses:**
- Can serve registered agent
- Can serve officer or managing agent
- Can serve employee at principal place of business
- Refusal by receptionist doesn't prevent service on authorized person

**Special Rules:**
- Secretary of State has registered agent information
- Businesses must maintain current registered agent
- Service on any authorized person is valid

### Government Entities

**When Serving Government:**
- Must follow specific statutory procedures
- Often require service on specific officers
- May have immunity issues
- Consult attorney for proper method

### Protected Individuals

**Active Military:**
- Servicemembers Civil Relief Act protections
- Special service requirements
- Commanding officer notification may be required

**Incarcerated Individuals:**
- Service through correctional facility
- Warden or designated agent
- Follow facility procedures

**Minors:**
- Service on parent or guardian
- Special rules for emancipated minors
- Different timeframes may apply

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Can a defendant be arrested for refusing service?

**A:** Generally no, simply refusing to accept papers is not a crime. However, if the refusal involves threats, assault, or interference with a court order, criminal charges could apply.

### Q: What if the defendant throws the papers back?

**A:** Once dropped at their feet and identified, service is complete. What they do with the papers afterward doesn't invalidate service. The server should document that papers were dropped and identified.

### Q: Can a process server force a defendant to take papers?

**A:** No. Process servers cannot use force. They can only drop papers at the defendant's feet if refused. Physical force would be illegal and invalidate the service.

### Q: What if the defendant runs away?

**A:** If the server was in the defendant's presence, identified themselves, and stated they had papers, service may be complete depending on circumstances. Generally, if the defendant actively flees to avoid service, the server can:
- Pursue briefly if safe to do so
- Complete drop service if papers can be placed near defendant
- Document the evasion attempt
- Use alternative service methods

### Q: Does the defendant have to sign anything?

**A:** No. Oklahoma law does not require a signature for valid service. Some process servers use electronic signature pads for their records, but this is not legally required.

### Q: What if the defendant claims they never got the papers?

**A:** This is why documentation is crucial. Professional process servers provide:
- Detailed affidavits
- GPS coordinates
- Timestamps
- Photographic evidence
- Witness statements (if available)

This documentation typically defeats false claims of non-service.

### Q: Can service be completed by certified mail if refused in person?

**A:** Certified mail is an alternative method, but the defendant must sign for it. If they're refusing personal service, they likely won't sign for certified mail either. Drop service is usually more effective.

### Q: What should I do if someone refuses service on me?

**A:** If you're the plaintiff and the defendant refuses:
1. Hire a professional process server (they're trained for this)
2. Ensure they're documenting everything
3. Consider skip tracing if defendant is hiding
4. Be prepared for alternative service methods

### Q: How many times will a process server try if refused?

**A:** Professional servers typically make 3-4 attempts at different times. If defendant consistently refuses or avoids, they may:
- Use drop service at first opportunity
- Attempt substitute service
- Recommend service by publication (if applicable)

### Q: Can I video record someone refusing service?

**A:** Oklahoma is a one-party consent state for recording. However, process servers should:
- Check local laws
- Notify they're recording (if required)
- Use body cameras if permitted
- Follow company policy
- Be aware of privacy expectations

Refusal to accept service papers doesn't prevent service—it simply changes the method used. Oklahoma law provides clear procedures for completing service even when defendants actively resist.
