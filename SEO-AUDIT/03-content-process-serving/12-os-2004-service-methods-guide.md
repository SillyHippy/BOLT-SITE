# Oklahoma Process Serving Methods: Complete Guide to 12 O.S. § 2004

**Understanding the legal framework for service of process in Oklahoma civil litigation**

---

Proper service of process is the cornerstone of due process in Oklahoma civil litigation. Without valid service, a court cannot exercise personal jurisdiction over a defendant, rendering any subsequent judgment unenforceable. Title 12, Section 2004 of the Oklahoma Statutes establishes comprehensive procedures governing how legal documents must be delivered to parties in civil cases. This guide provides a detailed examination of Oklahoma process serving methods, including personal service, substitute service, service by publication, and the procedural requirements that ensure compliance with state law.

## Overview of 12 O.S. § 2004

Oklahoma's process serving framework is codified in **12 O.S. § 2004**, which governs the issuance, form, and methods of serving summons and petitions in civil actions. The statute provides multiple avenues for service, recognizing that defendants may be located in various circumstances that require flexible yet constitutionally sound approaches.

> **Statutory Authority:** "Upon filing of the petition, the clerk shall forthwith issue a summons. Upon request of the plaintiff separate or additional summons shall issue against any defendants." — 12 O.S. § 2004(A)

The statute emphasizes that service must be reasonably calculated to provide actual notice of proceedings and an opportunity to be heard, reflecting the constitutional due process requirements established by the United States Supreme Court.

## Who May Serve Process in Oklahoma

Oklahoma law provides three categories of individuals authorized to serve civil process:

### 1. Sheriff or Deputy Sheriff
County sheriffs and their deputies serve as the traditional officers for process service. When serving as a sheriff or deputy, the officer must deposit service vouchers in the Sheriff's Service Fee Account as established under 19 O.S. § 514.1.

### 2. Licensed Private Process Servers
Oklahoma requires private process servers to obtain a license under **12 O.S. § 158.1**. Licensed servers must:
- Be at least 18 years of age
- Demonstrate good moral character
- Maintain a $5,000.00 performance bond
- Carry their license while on duty

### 3. Specially Appointed Persons
The court may freely make special appointments to serve process when circumstances warrant. This provision ensures that service can be accomplished even when traditional methods are impractical.

> **Statutory Text:** "At the election of the plaintiff, process, other than a subpoena, shall be served by a sheriff or deputy sheriff, a person licensed to make service of process in civil cases or a person specially appointed for that purpose. The court shall freely make special appointments to serve all process, other than a subpoena, under this paragraph." — 12 O.S. § 2004(C)(1)(a)

## Method 1: Personal Service (Personal Delivery)

Personal service remains the gold standard for service of process, providing the highest level of assurance that a defendant has received actual notice of legal proceedings.

### Service Upon Individuals

For individual defendants who are not minors under 15 or incompetent persons, **12 O.S. § 2004(C)(1)(c)(1)** provides several acceptable methods:

> **Statutory Text:** "upon an individual other than an infant who is less than fifteen (15) years of age or an incompetent person, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition personally or by leaving copies thereof at the person's dwelling house or usual place of abode with some person then residing therein who is fifteen (15) years of age or older, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition to an agent authorized by appointment or by law to receive service of process, or by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition personally or by leaving copies thereof at an agreed meeting place with some person then residing at the person's dwelling house or usual place of abode"

This provision establishes four primary methods of personal service:

1. **Direct Personal Delivery:** Handing the documents directly to the defendant
2. **Substitute Service at Dwelling:** Leaving copies at the defendant's residence with a competent person (15 years or older) who resides there
3. **Service on Authorized Agent:** Delivering to an agent legally authorized to accept service
4. **Agreed Meeting Place Service:** Delivery at a mutually agreed location

### Service Upon Minors

Service upon infants under 15 years of age requires special procedures under **12 O.S. § 2004(C)(1)(c)(2)**:

> **Statutory Text:** "upon an infant who is less than fifteen (15) years of age, by serving the summons and petition personally and upon either of the infant's parents or guardian, or if they cannot be found, then upon the person having the care or control of the infant or with whom the infant lives"

This dual-service requirement ensures that minors have appropriate adult representation when named in litigation.

### Service Upon Incompetent Persons

Similarly, service upon incompetent persons requires service on both the individual and their guardian:

> **Statutory Text:** "upon an incompetent person by serving the summons and petition personally and upon the incompetent person's guardian"

### Service Upon Entities

**Corporations and Partnerships:** Service upon business entities requires delivery to specific authorized individuals:

> **Statutory Text:** "upon a domestic or foreign corporation or upon a partnership or other unincorporated association which is subject to suit under a common name, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition to an officer, a managing or general agent or to any other agent authorized by appointment or by law to receive service of process"

**Governmental Entities:** Service upon state and local governmental organizations requires delivery to designated officials:

> **Statutory Text:** "upon a state, county, school district, public trust or municipal corporation or other governmental organization thereof subject to suit, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition to the officer or individual designated by specific statute; however, if there is no statute, then upon the chief executive officer or a clerk, secretary or other official whose duty it is to maintain the official records of the organization"

### Service Upon Incarcerated Persons

**Department of Corrections Inmates:**
> **Statutory Text:** "upon an inmate incarcerated in an institution under the jurisdiction and control of the Department of Corrections, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition to the warden or superintendent or the designee of the warden or superintendent of the institution where the inmate is housed. It shall be the duty of the receiving warden or superintendent or a designee to promptly deliver the summons and petition to the inmate named therein."

**County Jail Inmates:**
> **Statutory Text:** "upon an inmate incarcerated in a county jail or detention center under the jurisdiction and control of the county sheriff or the jail trust of the county, by delivering a copy of the summons and of the petition to the jail or detention center administrator or the designee of such administrator"

## Method 2: Service by Mail

When personal delivery is impractical or when the plaintiff elects this method, **12 O.S. § 2004(C)(2)** authorizes service by certified mail with specific procedural requirements.

### Eligible Defendants

Service by mail may be used for:
- Individuals (as defined in C(1)(c)(1))
- Corporations and partnerships (as defined in C(1)(c)(3))
- Governmental entities (as defined in C(1)(c)(5))

### Statutory Requirements

> **Statutory Text:** "Service by mail shall be accomplished by mailing a copy of the summons and petition by certified mail, return receipt requested and delivery restricted to the addressee."

The statute establishes strict requirements for mail service:

1. **Certified Mail:** Documents must be sent via certified mail
2. **Return Receipt Requested:** The sender must request confirmation of delivery
3. **Restricted Delivery:** Delivery must be restricted to the named addressee
4. **Separate Envelopes:** When multiple defendants exist, each must receive documents in separate envelopes

### Service Timing and Effectiveness

> **Statutory Text:** "Service by mail shall be effective on the date of receipt or if refused, on the date of refusal of the summons and petition by the defendant."

This provision establishes that service is complete upon the defendant's receipt or refusal, not when the documents are mailed.

### Default Judgment Limitations

The statute imposes specific requirements before default judgments may be entered based on mail service:

> **Statutory Text:** "Service by mail shall not be the basis for the entry of a default or a judgment by default unless the record contains a return receipt showing acceptance by the defendant or a returned envelope showing refusal of the process by the defendant."

### Refusal of Service Procedure

When a defendant refuses certified mail delivery, special procedures apply:

> **Statutory Text:** "If delivery of the process is refused, upon the receipt of notice of such refusal and at least ten (10) days before applying for entry of default, the person elected by plaintiff pursuant to subparagraph a of this paragraph to serve the process shall mail to the defendant by first-class mail a copy of the summons and petition and a notice prepared by the plaintiff that despite such refusal the case will proceed and that judgment by default will be rendered against him unless he appears to defend the suit."

## Method 3: Service by Publication

When traditional service methods fail despite diligent efforts, **12 O.S. § 2004(C)(3)** provides for service by publication as a last resort.

### When Publication is Authorized

Service by publication is permitted only under specific circumstances:

> **Statutory Text:** "Service of summons upon a named defendant may be made by publication when it is stated in the petition, verified by the plaintiff or the plaintiff's attorney or in a separate affidavit by the plaintiff or the plaintiff's attorney filed with the court, that with due diligence service cannot be made upon the defendant by any other method."

The statute also permits service by publication for:
- Unknown successors of named defendants
- Named decedents
- Dissolved partnerships, corporations, or associations

### Publication Requirements

> **Statutory Text:** "Service pursuant to this paragraph shall be made by publication of a notice, signed by the court clerk, one (1) day a week for three (3) consecutive weeks in a newspaper authorized by law to publish legal notices which is published in the county where the petition is filed."

Key requirements include:
- Publication once weekly for three consecutive weeks
- Publication in a newspaper authorized to publish legal notices
- The newspaper must be published in the county where the petition is filed (or adjoining county if none exists locally)

### Content Requirements for Publication Notice

The publication notice must contain:
- The court where the petition is filed
- Names of plaintiffs and parties served by publication
- Designation of parties whose unknown successors are being served
- Statement that defendants have been sued and must answer
- Deadline for response (not less than 41 days from first publication)
- Nature of the judgment that will be rendered

### Due Diligence Requirement

Before entering a default judgment based on publication service, courts must conduct an inquiry:

> **Statutory Text:** "Before entry of a default judgment or order against a party who has been served solely by publication under this paragraph, the court shall conduct an inquiry to determine whether the plaintiff, or someone acting in behalf of the plaintiff, made a distinct and meaningful search of all reasonably available sources to ascertain the whereabouts of any named parties who have been served solely by publication under this paragraph."

### Setting Aside Publication Service Judgments

A unique provision allows defendants to challenge default judgments entered by publication within three years:

> **Statutory Text:** "A party against whom a default judgment or order has been rendered, without other service than by publication in a newspaper, may, at any time within three (3) years after the filing of the judgment or order, have the judgment or order set aside in the manner prescribed in Sections 1031.1 and 1033 of this title."

## Method 4: Service on the Secretary of State

**12 O.S. § 2004(C)(4)** provides a specialized method for serving corporations when traditional methods fail.

### When Secretary of State Service is Permitted

> **Statutory Text:** "Service of process on a domestic or foreign corporation may be made by serving the Secretary of State as the corporation's agent, if:
> 1. there is no registered agent for the corporation listed in the records of the Secretary of State, or
> 2. neither the registered agent nor an officer of the corporation could be found at the registered office of the corporation, when service of process was attempted."

### Prerequisites for Service

Before resorting to Secretary of State service, plaintiffs must attempt service at:
- The corporation's last-known address on Franchise Tax Division records
- The corporation's last-known address on Secretary of State records
- The corporation's last address known to the plaintiff

### Procedure

> **Statutory Text:** "Service on the Secretary of State shall be made by filing two (2) copies of the summons and petition with the Secretary of State, notifying the Secretary of State that service is being made pursuant to the provisions of this paragraph, and paying the Secretary of State the fee prescribed in paragraph 7 of subsection A of Section 1142 of Title 18 of the Oklahoma Statutes"

### Extended Answer Period

Corporations served through the Secretary of State receive extended time to respond:

> **Statutory Text:** "The corporation shall not be required to serve its answer until forty (40) days after service of the summons and petition on the Secretary of State."

## Method 5: Service by Acknowledgment

**12 O.S. § 2004(C)(5)** recognizes voluntary acceptance of service:

> **Statutory Text:** "An acknowledgment on the back of the summons or the voluntary appearance of a defendant is equivalent to service."

This method is often used when defendants waive formal service to reduce costs and expedite proceedings.

## Method 6: Alternative Service Methods

When all traditional methods fail, **12 O.S. § 2004(C)(6)** allows courts to authorize alternative methods:

> **Statutory Text:** "If service cannot be made by personal delivery or by mail, a defendant of any class referred to in division (1) or (3) of subparagraph c of paragraph 1 of this subsection may be served as provided by court order in a manner which is reasonably calculated to give the defendant actual notice of the proceedings and an opportunity to be heard"

This provision requires:
- Filing an affidavit demonstrating due diligence
- Court approval of the proposed alternative method
- Methods reasonably calculated to provide actual notice

## Proof of Service Requirements

**12 O.S. § 2004(G)** establishes comprehensive requirements for proving service:

### Return Affidavit Requirements

> **Statutory Text:** "The person serving the process shall make proof of service thereof to the court promptly and in any event within the time during which the person served must respond to the process"

### Content of Return

The return must include:
- County of issuance
- Name of person served
- Date of service
- Place of service
- Method of service

### Service by Mail Documentation

> **Statutory Text:** "If service was by mail, the person mailing the summons and petition shall endorse on the copy of the summons or order of the court that is filed in the action the date and place of mailing and the date when service was receipted or service was rejected, and shall attach to the copy of the summons or order a copy of the return receipt or returned envelope"

## Time Limits for Service

**12 O.S. § 2004(I)** establishes critical time limitations:

> **Statutory Text:** "If service of process is not made upon a defendant within one hundred eighty (180) days after the filing of the petition and the plaintiff has not shown good cause why such service was not made within that period, the action shall be deemed dismissed as to that defendant without prejudice"

Key points:
- **180-day deadline** for serving process
- Action deemed dismissed without prejudice if deadline missed
- Court must enter dismissal order within 200 days if no service on any defendant
- Exceptions exist for defendants outside Oklahoma for 180 days

## Amendment of Process or Proof

**12 O.S. § 2004(H)** provides flexibility for correcting errors:

> **Statutory Text:** "At any time in its discretion and upon such terms as it deems just, the court may allow any process or proof of service thereof to be amended, unless it clearly appears that material prejudice would result to the substantial rights of the party against whom the process issued."

## Conclusion

Oklahoma's process serving framework under **12 O.S. § 2004** provides a comprehensive system that balances the plaintiff's need to commence litigation with the defendant's constitutional right to notice and opportunity to be heard. Understanding these statutory requirements is essential for legal practitioners, process servers, and litigants to ensure valid service and avoid dismissal of actions for procedural defects.

The statute's tiered approach—beginning with personal service, progressing through mail service, and culminating in publication—ensures that plaintiffs have multiple avenues to effect service while maintaining due process protections. Careful attention to the specific procedural requirements for each method is critical to establishing valid jurisdiction and obtaining enforceable judgments.
