## About Creek County, Oklahoma

Creek County, established at Oklahoma statehood in 1907, is named after the Creek (Muscogee) Nation, whose tribal lands encompassed much of the area. Located in the heart of northeastern Oklahoma, Creek County covers 970 square miles of rolling hills, fertile plains, and historic communities that blend Native American heritage with modern growth.

The county seat, Sapulpa, serves as the administrative and judicial center for Creek County. With a population of approximately 71,000 residents spread across both urban centers and rural landscapes, Creek County offers a unique mix of suburban convenience, small-town charm, and agricultural tradition.

## Local Landmarks and Geography

### Turner Turnpike
The **Turner Turnpike** (Interstate 44) bisects Creek County, providing the primary transportation corridor connecting Tulsa to Oklahoma City. Opened in 1953 as Oklahoma's first turnpike, this 88-mile toll road features interchanges at Bristow and near Sapulpa/Kellyville, making travel throughout the county efficient for both residents and process servers.

**Travel Times:**
- Tulsa to Sapulpa: ~20 minutes (14 miles)
- Tulsa to Bristow: ~35 minutes (25 miles)
- Sapulpa to Bristow: ~20 minutes (15 miles)
- Oklahoma City to Sapulpa: ~75 minutes

### Historic Route 66
**Historic Route 66** parallels much of the Turner Turnpike through Creek County, passing through Sapulpa, Kellyville, and Bristow. This iconic highway offers an alternative scenic route and serves many local businesses and residential areas.

### Heyburn Lake
**Heyburn Lake**, managed by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, is a 920-acre reservoir located near Kellyville in southern Creek County. The lake offers:

- **Camping facilities** at Sunset Bay, Sheppard Point, and Heyburn Park
- **Boating and water skiing**
- **Fishing** for crappie, bass, and catfish
- **Public hunting areas** covering 6,000+ acres
- **Hiking and equestrian trails**

The lake area includes both developed recreational facilities and rural residences that may require specialized service approaches.

### Keystone Lake
**Keystone Lake**, a much larger reservoir on the Arkansas River, forms part of Creek County's southern boundary. This 23,600-acre lake offers extensive recreational opportunities and serves as an important geographical landmark for the region.

### Oil and Gas Heritage Sites
Creek County played a pivotal role in Oklahoma's oil boom:

- **Glenn Pool Field** (near Mounds): Site of the 1905 oil discovery that launched Oklahoma's petroleum industry
- **Drumright Field**: One of Oklahoma's most prolific oil fields
- **Cushing**: Known as the "Pipeline Crossroads of the World," located just north of Creek County

These historic oil fields continue to influence the local economy and geography, with many rural residents employed in the petroleum industry.
