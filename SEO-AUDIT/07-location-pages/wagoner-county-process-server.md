## About Wagoner County, Oklahoma

### Historical Significance

Wagoner County was established on **July 16, 1907**, just months before Oklahoma achieved statehood. The county was named after **Henry "Bigfoot" Wagoner**, a prominent figure in the region's early development. At statehood in 1907, Wagoner's population was 2,950, and the existing federal courthouse gave the community the edge needed to be named county seat.

**Historical Distinction:** Wagoner holds the honor of being the **first incorporated town in Indian Territory**, achieving incorporation on **January 4, 1896**, under statutes adopted from Arkansas law.

### Geographic Overview

Wagoner County covers approximately **591 square miles** in northeastern Oklahoma:

- **County Seat**: Wagoner
- **Population**: Approximately 80,000 residents (2020 Census)
- **Location**: East of Creek County, north of Muskogee County
- **Major Cities**: Wagoner, Coweta, Porter, Okay, Redbird, Toppers
- **Waterways**: Fort Gibson Lake, Grand River, Verdigris River, Arkansas River

### Creek County Neighbor

Wagoner County shares a border with Creek County to the west, connected by:
- **U.S. Highway 266** ( overlap with Historic Route 66)
- **State Highway 51** through Broken Arrow
- **Rural roads** connecting unincorporated areas

## Wagoner County Courthouse

### Historic Courthouse Building

**Address**: 307 E. Cherokee Street, Wagoner, OK 74467  
**Phone**: (918) 485-2216  
**Hours**: Monday–Friday, 8:00 AM – 4:30 PM

The **Wagoner County Courthouse** is a historic WPA-era building constructed in 1939–1940:

**Construction History:**
- **July 12, 1938**: Voters approved 5-mill tax increase for courthouse construction
- **Architect**: Jos. I. Davis
- **Funding**: Works Progress Administration (WPA) federal funds
- **Completion**: 1940
- **Style**: Art Deco/Moderne influences typical of WPA architecture

The courthouse is listed on the **National Register of Historic Places** and represents one of Oklahoma's finest examples of Depression-era public architecture.
